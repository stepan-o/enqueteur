# Enquêteur Visual Style Canon v0.2

Status: provisional canon, actively locked for implementation  
Purpose: establish the current visual source of truth for Enquêteur after locking the new primary logo  
Scope: brand identity, shell styling, character presentation direction, environment graphic language, motion tone, and prompt alignment

---

## 1. Intent

Enquêteur should feel like a **literary Montréal night mystery**.

The visual language is:

- nocturnal
- cultured
- theatrical
- urban
- refined
- painterly
- slightly magical
- emotionally intelligent
- modern, but not sterile

The target feeling is:

**Montréal at night under gallery lighting.**

The game should not feel like generic detective noir, gothic horror, flat casual mobile mystery, or fantasy occult intrigue.  
It should feel civic, elegant, atmospheric, and human.

---

## 2. Current primary brand conclusion

The currently locked provisional primary brand mark is the gold **Enquêteur** wordmark over a nocturnal indigo/plum painted field with Montréal skyline silhouette, ember glow, celestial glints, and fleur-de-lis accent.

This logo is now the strongest brand anchor for post-H work.

It establishes Enquêteur less as a collage of detective symbols and more as a **wordmark-led literary civic nocturne**.

That shift is intentional.

The brand center of gravity is now:

- literary rather than gadget-like
- cultural rather than gamey
- Montréal-specific rather than generic mystery
- elegant rather than icon-busy
- atmospheric rather than cluttered

## 4. Core style statement

Enquêteur’s style is:

**gold-lit literary mystery over Montréal’s skyline, suspended in indigo night and museum hush**

Operationally, this means the game should combine:

- a premium literary wordmark identity
- painterly human drama
- civic and cultural atmosphere
- museum-night wayfinding intelligence
- restrained symbolic magic
- warm focal light against cool urban darkness

A shorter working compression:

**literary civic nocturne**

---

## 5. Mood pillars

These emotional pillars should guide all visual decisions.

### 5.1 Nocturnal
Night is the foundational condition of the brand.

Night in Enquêteur should feel:
- inhabited
- illuminated in chosen places
- atmospheric
- urban
- elegant
- alive with significance

Night should not feel:
- empty
- muddy
- oppressive by default
- horror-coded

### 5.2 Cultured
The world must feel shaped by:
- museums
- archives
- civic institutions
- exhibition design
- literary framing
- careful materials
- urban memory

It should carry intelligence and taste.

### 5.3 Theatrical
The game should feel dramatically lit, but controlled:
- warm focal emphasis
- selective highlights
- visual pauses and hush
- deliberate staging of faces, clues, and spaces

This is theatrical in the sense of **gallery lighting, poster composition, and literary drama**, not camp performance.

### 5.4 Urban
Enquêteur belongs to a real city.

Urban cues should include:
- skyline silhouettes
- civic architecture
- signs and routes
- winter clothing and practical objects
- lamps, thresholds, streets, buildings
- Montréal-specific cultural signals where useful

### 5.5 Slightly magical
The “magic” in Enquêteur is atmospheric rather than supernatural:
- ember light
- tiny celestial glints
- symbolic hush
- painterly bloom
- visual significance

This should feel poetic, not fantasy-worldbuilding-heavy.

### 5.6 Human
Even with cultural atmosphere and symbolic framing, the emotional core is still human:
- faces matter
- tension matters
- trust and unease matter
- silence matters
- reading people matters

## 6. Palette canon

### 6.1 Palette family

The core Enquêteur palette remains a **midnight jewel palette**, now with a stronger gold-led brand hierarchy:

#### Structural darks
- deep indigo
- velvet plum
- smoky aubergine
- midnight blue-black

#### Warm focal lights
- gallery gold
- amber glow
- saffron light
- ember copper
- restrained vermilion ember

#### Readable neutrals
- parchment ivory
- warm dust
- smoke mauve

#### Limited secondary accent
- petrol teal / fleur teal, used sparingly

### 6.2 Palette logic

#### Cool structural family
Use for:
- page and shell background
- panel depth
- night atmosphere
- shadow structure
- environmental massing
- secondary framing surfaces

#### Warm focal family
Use for:
- title emphasis
- important actions
- active highlights
- signal points
- lamp-like warmth
- investigation emphasis
- controlled glow

#### Neutral family
Use for:
- readable text
- gentle contrast
- warm labels
- muted support content
- dividers and softened hierarchy

### 6.3 Palette behavior rules

- Gold/amber should function as **light and emphasis**, not blanket decoration.
- Indigo/plum should carry most structural weight.
- Vermilion should be rarer than gold/amber.
- Teal should remain restrained and secondary.
- Ivory should stay warm, never cold digital white.
- Warm light should appear intentional, never sprayed everywhere.

### 6.4 Brand color priority
If unsure, favor this order:

1. indigo / plum structure
2. gold / amber emphasis
3. ivory text
4. vermilion tension accents
5. teal as secondary counterpoint

## 7. Material and texture logic

The world should feel rich, but not overworked.

Preferred material cues:
- dark lacquer or enamel
- softly painted surfaces
- brass-like warmth
- matte institutional finishes
- paper and placard textures
- gently worn civic surfaces
- museum signage logic
- atmospheric grain
- soft skyline haze

Texture rules:
- painterly grain is welcome
- atmosphere is welcome
- subtle distress is welcome
- visible surface life is welcome

Avoid:
- muddy grime
- cheap grunge overlays
- glossy candy-game finish
- hyper-clean sterile vector flatness
- faux-antique fantasy parchment overload

---

## 8. Rendering philosophy

### 8.1 Readability comes first
Everything must remain gameplay-legible:
- actions clear
- labels readable
- clues recognizable
- faces readable
- object silhouette preserved
- space understandable

### 8.2 Painterly, not vague
Painterly means:
- expressive
- atmospheric
- textured
- human
- warm/cool responsive

Painterly does not mean:
- blurry
- mushy
- low-contrast
- impressionistic to the point of gameplay loss

### 8.3 Gold light against indigo night
This is now the central brand lighting rule:
- cool nocturnal field
- warm illuminated focality

That contrast should shape:
- shell screens
- title presentation
- portraits
- map treatment
- panels
- later environment work

### 8.4 Elegant restraint
Enquêteur should feel curated, not overloaded.
Decorative language must support:
- hierarchy
- atmosphere
- distinction
- legibility

### 8.5 Wordmark-led identity
The logo now tells us that Enquêteur should lead with:
- strong title treatment
- strong tonal silhouette
- selective motifs
- cultural confidence

Not every surface needs multiple icons or detective symbolism.

### 9.1 Not cyberpunk
Avoid:
- neon overload
- tech-futurist glow logic

- glitch aesthetics

### 9.2 Not casual mystery mobile
Avoid:
- bubbly buttons
- toy-like shapes
- bright candy colors
- overfriendly generic mobile polish

### 9.3 Not horror-goth
Avoid:
- monster-coded darkness
- occult-heavy symbolism
- blood-red dominance
- aggressive haunted-house framing

### 9.4 Not sterile museum minimalism
Avoid:
- white-box institutional coldness
- bland editorial neutrality
- generic cultural brochure branding

### 9.5 Not detective cliché overload
Avoid:
- hat-and-pipe parody

- clue-collage clutter

Avoid:
- medieval scroll framing

Avoid:
- brown-gray sludge

## 10. UI rules

The UI should feel like:

**gallery signage meets literary notebook**

and now, more specifically:

**museum-night title card meets investigation interface**

### 10.1 Shell and panel identity
Panels should feel:
- dark
- layered
- softly luminous
- composed
- modern
- slightly ceremonial

Panels may use:
- soft indigo/plum depth
- gold-edged emphasis
- warm hover light
- subtle shadow and haze
- refined borders
- controlled texture

### 10.2 Hierarchy
The hierarchy should clearly distinguish:
- title / identity layer
- location / situation layer
- decision/action layer
- supporting narrative layer
- warnings/contradictions/discoveries
- debug/secondary diagnostics

### 10.3 Button language
Buttons should feel like:
- elegant plaques
- refined callouts
- deliberate actions
- lit functional objects

Preferred behavior:
- dark body
- warm emphasis
- clear state changes
- readable in both EN and FR

### 10.4 Text presentation
Text should feel:
- literary but practical
- warm and readable
- atmospheric without becoming ornate

Use:
- display serif for high-identity moments
- clean contemporary sans for UI/support text

### 10.5 Signage cues
Wayfinding, direction, and emphasis can borrow from:
- gallery labels
- museum plaques
- route markers
- skyline and civic silhouette logic
- subtle celestial glints
- cultural signage restraint

### 10.6 Gold usage rule
Gold is not just decoration.  
Gold should mean:
- title
- emphasis
- active invitation
- illuminated meaning

Do not let every surface glow equally.

## 11. Typography direction

### 11.1 Display type
Display typography should feel:
- literary
- elegant
- slightly classical
- cultured
- emotionally resonant
- premium without stiffness

### 11.2 UI/support type
UI type should feel:
- modern
- clean
- warm
- functional
- exhibition-supportive rather than app-dashboard cold

### 11.3 Hierarchy principle
Use:
- serif for title moments and high-identity headings
- sans for interactive clarity and support text

Avoid:
- overusing decorative serif in dense UI
- overly geometric tech-sans for everything
- playful casual typography

## 12. Symbol language

The current brand mark clarifies the symbol hierarchy.

### 12.1 Primary symbol family
These symbols are most aligned with the current brand:
- gold wordmark
- skyline silhouette
- fleur-de-lis
- celestial glints / small stars
- warm arc / sweep
- museum-night horizon line

### 12.2 Secondary symbol family
Use sparingly:
- plaques
- route markers
- arches
- cupolas
- bridge or civic structural silhouettes
- wayfinding arrows

### 12.3 De-emphasized symbols
Do not lead the identity with:
- literal detective tools
- busy clue clusters
- overly many object icons
- novelty mystery props

Investigation should increasingly read through:
- tone
- typography
- light
- composition
- civic symbolism

## 13. Portrait rules

Portraits remain central to the game’s emotional identity.

### 13.1 Portrait target
Portraits should be:
- painterly
- expressive
- psychologically readable
- restrained but specific
- warmly lit against cool shadow
- human and believable

### 13.2 Lighting
Portrait lighting should follow:
- cool structural darkness
- warm amber/saffron focal light
- selective coral/vermilion tension accents
- subdued, non-competing backgrounds

### 13.3 Finish level
Aim for:
- visible texture
- strong face readability
- nuanced expression
- realism with painterly life

Avoid:
- plastic cinematic polish
- muddy under-definition
- exaggerated cartoon simplification
- melodramatic overacting

### 13.4 State readability
Portrait states should read through:
- expression
- gaze
- posture
- warmth vs tension shift
- light shift
- controlled palette modulation

Not through oversized theatrical distortion.

---

## 14. Environment rules

The world should feel civic, architectural, and emotionally lit.

### 14.1 Spatial tone
Rooms and spaces should feel like:
- museum interiors
- cultural institution corridors
- service and staff zones
- urban thresholds
- secure but inhabited civic spaces
- night-lit architectural environments

### 14.2 Environment priorities
Order of priority:
1. spatial readability
2. object recognition
3. emotional atmosphere
4. decorative richness

### 14.3 Graphic language
Draw inspiration from:
- museum maps
- route highlights
- gallery signage
- skyline silhouettes
- institutional labels
- lit entry points
- directional cues
- cultural-space geometry

### 14.4 Color usage
Environment art should:
- rest primarily in cool structure
- reveal meaning through warm light
- reserve strongest warm accents for key locations and clue framing
- support investigation legibility first

### 14.5 Skyline rule
The skyline is a powerful identity cue, but should be used strategically.  
It should support:
- shell identity
- title moments
- transitions
- atmospheric framing

It should not be pasted everywhere indiscriminately.

---

## 15. Motion and feedback rules

Motion should feel like the brand:
- refined
- nocturnal
- warm
- restrained
- intelligent

### 15.1 Allowed motion language
Use:
- ember breathing glow
- tiny star-like glints
- soft skyline haze
- subtle ambient drift
- warm highlight travel
- low-amplitude fades and pulses

### 15.2 Avoid
Avoid:
- arcade bounce
- reward spam
- loud particle showers
- heavy flicker
- constant motion everywhere
- casino/mobile-game animation language

### 15.3 Motion purpose
Motion should support:
- atmosphere
- focus
- transition quality
- tactile feedback
- emotional hush

It should not compete with interpretation or reading.

---

## 16. Bilingual presentation rule

Because EN/FR support is part of the baseline product:

- layouts must tolerate text-length differences
- title framing must survive longer French copy
- panels must not depend on short English assumptions
- buttons must remain readable under French expansion
- decorative shell treatments must never compromise usability
- any new text added during post-H must align with localization architecture

Style never outranks bilingual clarity.
