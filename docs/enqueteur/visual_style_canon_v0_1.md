# Enquêteur Visual Style Canon v0.1

Status: provisional canon  
Purpose: establish the first shared visual source of truth for Enquêteur’s post-H presentation work  
Scope: UI identity, shell styling, character presentation direction, environment graphic language, motion tone, and future asset/prompt alignment

---

## 1. Intent

Enquêteur should feel like a **cultured urban mystery at night**.

The visual language is:

- nocturnal
- theatrical
- cultured
- urban
- slightly magical
- painterly, but readable
- dramatic, but not horror
- elegant, but not sterile

The target feeling is:

**Montréal at night under gallery lighting.**

This is not generic detective noir, not gothic horror, and not flat casual puzzle-game UI.  
It should feel civic, intelligent, atmospheric, and human.

## 3. Core style statement

Enquêteur’s style is:

**museum-night mystery + painterly human drama + graphic wayfinding intelligence**

This means the game should combine:

- the warmth and ceremony of cultural spaces
- the emotional intimacy of painted literary portraits
- the visual clarity of signs, clues, routes, and spatial logic
- the mystery of night, but with intelligence rather than grime

---

## 4. Mood pillars

The following emotional pillars should guide all art and presentation work.

### 4.1 Nocturnal
The game happens in a visual world shaped by night:
- deep indigo foundations
- cool shadow structure
- selective warm light
- strong contrast between darkness and illuminated focal points

Night should feel inhabited and alive, not empty or terrifying.

### 4.2 Theatrical
Scenes should feel staged in a subtle way:
- focused pools of light
- deliberate visual emphasis
- dramatic but controlled composition
- a sense that every object and face is lit for attention

This is theatrical in the sense of **gallery lighting and dramatic framing**, not stage camp.

### 4.3 Cultured
The world should feel informed by:
- museums
- archives
- civic institutions
- elegant typography
- signage systems
- thoughtful materials
- historical continuity

Nothing should feel cheap, disposable, or juvenile.

### 4.4 Urban
The world is a real city, not a fantasy kingdom:
- stone, glass, signage, streets, lamps, routes
- practical coats, scarves, cups, benches, doors
- civic geometry and wayfinding
- Montréal specificity where appropriate

Urban means grounded, not generic metropolitan slickness.

### 4.5 Slightly magical
There may be a sense of glow, hush, significance, and almost-ritual atmosphere:
- ember light
- painterly bloom
- subtle shimmer
- quiet symbolic emphasis

But this should remain restrained.  
The game is not supernatural fantasy.  
The “magic” is emotional and atmospheric.

## 5. Palette canon

### 5.1 Palette family

The core Enquêteur palette is a **midnight jewel palette**:

- deep indigo
- smoky plum
- aubergine
- petrol teal
- warm amber
- saffron
- ember coral
- vermilion
- parchment ivory

### 5.2 Palette logic

#### Cool structural colors
Use these for:
- page background
- shell background
- night atmosphere
- panel depth
- shadow structure
- environmental base masses

Primary cool family:
- deep indigo
- smoky plum
- aubergine
- petrol teal

#### Warm focal colors
Use these for:
- important actions
- title emphasis
- lamps / lit windows / glow accents
- key symbols
- investigative highlights
- visual cues requiring attention

Primary warm family:
- warm amber
- saffron
- ember coral
- vermilion

#### Readability color
Use parchment ivory for:
- primary readable text
- title lettering
- elegant high-contrast highlights
- icon fill in key spots

Ivory should feel warm and paper-like, not stark digital white.

### 5.3 Palette behavior rules

- Warm colors should read as **light, signal, or emphasis**, not blanket fill.
- Cool colors should carry the structural mass of the interface and environments.
- Teal is a supporting counter-accent, not the dominant brand hue.
- Vermilion/coral should be used more sparingly than amber/saffron.
- Ivory should preserve legibility and elegance, not flatten contrast.

## 6. Material and texture logic

The game should feel materially rich without becoming noisy.

Preferred material cues:
- dark lacquered surfaces
- paper and card
- brass or warm metallic trims
- softly worn stone
- painted signage
- glass glow
- matte institutional surfaces
- subtle grain

Texture rules:
- visible texture is welcome
- painterly grain is welcome
- lightly distressed surfaces are welcome
- avoid dirty mud or hyper-grunge
- avoid sterile vector flatness
- avoid glossy mobile-game candy finish

---

## 7. Rendering philosophy

### 7.1 Readable first
Everything must remain gameplay-legible:
- actions must be clear
- text must be readable
- characters must be recognizable
- evidence objects must remain understandable
- environment silhouettes must support navigation

### 7.2 Painterly, not blurry
Painterly does not mean vague.
The target is:
- expressive
- textured
- human
- atmospheric
- but still crisp enough for gameplay decisions

### 7.3 Warm against cool
The central lighting logic is:
- cool nocturnal structure
- warm selective illumination

This contrast should shape portraits, shell screens, map graphics, and environment details.

### 7.4 Elegant restraint
The world should feel curated, not overloaded.
Decorative language must support:
- clarity
- rhythm
- tone
- hierarchy

Do not over-ornament the UI.

---

## 8. Anti-targets

Enquêteur should **not** drift toward any of the following:

### 8.1 Not cyberpunk
Avoid:
- neon overload
- electric magenta/cyan nightclub palette
- hyper-tech futurism
- glitch-heavy UI

### 8.2 Not flat casual mobile
Avoid:
- toy-like buttons
- bubbly panels
- oversimplified iconography
- colorful generic puzzle-game polish

### 8.3 Not sterile corporate museum branding
Avoid:
- white-wall minimalism as the whole identity
- overly clean institutional modernism
- bland brochure aesthetics

### 8.4 Not fantasy parchment UI
Avoid:
- medieval scroll framing
- faux antique fantasy embellishment
- ornate “magic detective” clichés

### 8.5 Not muddy prestige realism
Avoid:
- brown-gray sludge palettes
- grim desaturation everywhere
- visual heaviness that kills readability

### 8.6 Not cute detective cartoon
Avoid:
- mascot-like whimsy
- exaggerated comedy mystery tone
- childish detective kitsch

## 9. UI rules

The UI should feel like:

**gallery signage meets detective notebook**

Not fantasy parchment.  
Not police terminal UI.  
Not a modern SaaS dashboard.

### 9.1 Shell and panel identity
Panels should feel:
- dark
- soft-edged
- grounded
- layered
- quietly luminous

Panels may use:
- subtle warm edge emphasis
- faint texture
- restrained shadow depth
- understated border logic

### 9.2 Hierarchy
The visual hierarchy should clearly distinguish:
- title / scene / location level
- active decision level
- supporting information
- warnings / contradictions / discoveries
- debug or secondary diagnostics

### 9.3 Button language
Buttons should:
- feel deliberate and elegant
- prioritize clarity over ornament
- use amber/saffron as primary action emphasis
- use vermilion/coral for danger or critical action
- remain robust in EN and FR

### 9.4 Text presentation
Text should feel literary but readable:
- primary content remains easy to scan
- decorative typography should not impair function
- ivory-on-dark remains the baseline
- muted text should stay warm, not dead gray

### 9.5 Signage cues
Wayfinding, direction, and investigative emphasis can borrow from:
- museum signage
- route markers
- directional plaques
- map-highlight logic

## 10. Portrait rules

Portraits are central to the emotional feel of Enquêteur.

### 10.1 Portrait target
Portraits should be:
- painterly
- expressive
- psychologically readable
- human
- slightly dramatic
- emotionally specific

### 10.2 Lighting
Portrait lighting should follow:
- cool structural shadow
- warm amber or saffron key light
- occasional coral/vermilion emotional accents
- subdued background fields

### 10.3 Finish level
Aim for:
- visible brush/texture feel
- strong face readability
- realistic proportions
- emotional nuance

Avoid:
- plastic cinematic realism
- airbrushed glamour
- cartoon simplification
- muddy under-defined faces

### 10.4 State readability
Portrait states should become legible through:
- light shifts
- posture shifts
- expression shifts
- controlled palette shifts
- tension vs warmth contrast

Do not turn state changes into melodramatic overacting.

## 11. Environment rules

The world should feel architectural, civic, and navigable.

### 11.1 Spatial tone
Rooms should feel like:
- museum interiors
- service spaces
- security-adjacent back-of-house areas
- cultural institution circulation zones
- urban evening thresholds

### 11.2 Environment art priorities
Order of priority:
1. spatial readability
2. object recognition
3. atmosphere
4. decorative richness

### 11.3 Graphic language
Use inspiration from:
- museum floor plans
- wayfinding systems
- signage plaques
- highlighted routes
- lit thresholds
- institutional labels

### 11.4 Color usage
Environments should:
- sit primarily in the cool family
- use warm light to reveal important areas
- reserve strongest warm accents for points of importance
- support clue readability rather than compete with it

---

## 12. Motion and feedback rules

Motion should feel restrained and intelligent.

### 12.1 Allowed motion language
Use:
- subtle glow breathing
- soft shimmer
- light drift
- dust / grain movement
- restrained highlight pulses
- low-amplitude transitions

### 12.2 Avoid
Avoid:
- arcade bounce
- loud particle spam
- constant pulsing everywhere
- gamey reward explosions
- frantic UI motion

### 12.3 Sound relation
If motion later pairs with sound, it should feel:
- tactile
- refined
- sparse
- atmospheric

Not casino-like or mobile-gamey.

---

## 13. Bilingual presentation rule

Because EN/FR support is part of product baseline:

- layouts must tolerate text-length differences
- shell identity must not rely on narrow English-only lockups
- button treatments must remain readable under French expansion
- panel rhythm must survive longer labels
- decorative framing must never compromise bilingual usability

Style should never overpower legibility in either language.

When unsure, prefer:

- cultured over flashy
- readable over ornate
- theatrical over generic
- urban over fantastical
- warm-lit mystery over dark grime
- emotional specificity over vague mood
